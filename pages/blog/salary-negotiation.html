Negotiating Your Next Tech Salary Hike

The exact phrases to use when discussing compensation with confidence

January 18, 2026 · 6 min read

Salary negotiation is where many talented engineers leave money on the table—not because they're not worth it, but because they don't know what to say. Here's your playbook.

The #1 Rule: Never Give a Number First

Whoever mentions a specific salary first loses negotiating power. Here's how to deflect:

When Asked: "What are your salary expectations?"

✅ Strong Response:

"I'm looking for a competitive package that reflects my experience and the value I'll bring to the team. What's the budget range for this role?"

Why it works: You're being professional while turning the question back to them.

Alternative (if pressed):

✅ Strong Response:

"Based on my research and the market rate for senior engineers with my skill set, I'm targeting the $X-Y range. But I'm flexible depending on the total compensation package, including equity and benefits."

Why it works: You give a range (not a fixed number) and show you're considering the full package.

Responding to a Low Offer

If the initial offer is below your expectations, don't panic or accept immediately. Use this framework:

The "Appreciate + Pause + Counter" Method

✅ Example:

"Thank you for the offer—I'm excited about the opportunity. However, based on my experience leading similar projects and the current market rates, I was expecting something closer to [higher number]. Is there flexibility in the compensation package?"

Power Phrases for Negotiation

Use These Phrases:

  • "Based on market research..." (shows you've done homework)
  • "Given my track record of..." (quantify your value)
  • "I'm confident I can deliver..." (future-focused)
  • "What's the total compensation package?" (think beyond base salary)
  • "Is there room for flexibility?" (opens negotiation)
  • "Can we revisit this in 6 months?" (if they can't budge now)

What NOT to Say

❌ Avoid These

  • "I need this salary because of personal expenses"
  • "My friend makes more"
  • "I'll accept if you can't go higher"
  • "I'm desperate for this job"
  • "What's the minimum you can offer?"

✅ Say This Instead

  • "Based on the value I bring..."
  • "Market data shows..."
  • "I need time to consider the full package"
  • "I'm very interested in this role"
  • "What's the maximum budget for this position?"

Negotiating Beyond Base Salary

If they can't increase base salary, negotiate these:

  • Signing bonus: "Can we bridge the gap with a one-time signing bonus?"
  • Stock options/RSUs: "Is there flexibility in the equity package?"
  • Performance review timeline: "Can we schedule a compensation review in 6 months?"
  • Remote work flexibility: "Would full remote work be possible?"
  • Learning budget: "Is there a professional development budget?"
  • Extra PTO: "Can we add a few more vacation days?"

The "Silence is Power" Technique

After you counter-offer, stop talking. The first person to speak usually loses. Let them process your request. The silence might feel uncomfortable, but it's working in your favor.

Pro Tip

Practice your negotiation script out loud before the actual conversation. It sounds awkward at first, but it builds confidence.

Handling Multiple Offers

If you have competing offers, use this leverage professionally:

✅ Professional Approach:

"I'm very interested in joining your team. I do have another offer on the table at [higher amount], but your company is my top choice because of [specific reason]. Is there any flexibility to match or come closer to that number?"

Why it works: You're being honest, showing genuine interest, and giving them a chance to compete.

When to Walk Away

Sometimes the best negotiation move is declining. Walk away if:

  • The offer is significantly below market rate and they won't budge
  • They're disrespectful during negotiation (red flag for company culture)
  • The role doesn't align with your career goals
  • You have a better offer elsewhere

How to decline professionally:

"Thank you for the offer and your time throughout this process. After careful consideration, I've decided to pursue another opportunity that's a better fit for my career goals at this time. I appreciate your understanding and hope our paths cross again in the future."

Key Takeaways

  • Never give a number first—ask for their range
  • Use the "Appreciate + Pause + Counter" method
  • Quantify your value with specific achievements
  • Think beyond base salary (equity, bonuses, PTO)
  • Practice your script before the conversation
  • Use silence as a negotiation tool
  • Be willing to walk away if needed
