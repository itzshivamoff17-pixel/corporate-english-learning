The Science of Attention: Why "Polite" Fails

The average knowledge worker receives 121 emails per day (Radicati Group). This means your email isn't being read; it's being scanned.

Jakob Nielsen's F-Shaped Pattern

Eye-tracking studies by the Nielsen Norman Group reveal that users read in an F-shaped pattern:

  • Top Bar: They read the first two lines fully.
  • Vertical Scan: They scan down the left side of the email.
  • The Void: They ignore the bottom right completely.

Conclusion: If your "Ask" is buried in paragraph 3, it doesn't exist.

Protocol 1: BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front)

The US Military uses a standard format for all messaging called BLUF. It respects the recipient's time by putting the conclusion first.

Protocol 2: eliminating "Zombie Phrases"

Old phrases waste cognitive load. Replace them with direct, active language.

The Zombie (Outdated) The Human (Modern) Why?
Please find attached... I've attached... / Here is... Active voice. "Find" implies a search mission.
I am writing to inform you... (Just say it) It is obvious you are writing. Don't announce it.
Per my last email... Re-surfacing this... "Per my last email" sounds passive-aggressive.
Kindly do the needful... Could you please [Action]? "Kindly" is often red as archaic or sarcastic in the US.

Protocol 3: The "One-Read" Rule

A concept from Harvard Business Review: An email should be written so that the recipient can answer it with one reading and one scroll. If they have to reply with "Clarifying Question?", you failed.

The 5 W's Checklist

Before hitting send, check if you answered these:

  • Who needs to act? (Use @mentions)
  • What is the action?
  • When is it due? (Deadlines drive priority)
  • Why is it important? (Context)
  • Where is the link/doc?
