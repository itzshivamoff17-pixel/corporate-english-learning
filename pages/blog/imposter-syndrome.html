Overcoming Imposter Syndrome in Tech

Why you feel like a fraud, and the exact scripts to reframe your mindset.

In a survey of 10,000 software engineers by the blind app, 58% reported experiencing imposter syndrome. Depending on who you ask at Google or Amazon, that number effectively rises to 80%.

You know the feeling: You're in a code review, and a Senior Engineer points out a flaw in your logic. Your heart sinks. Your brain immediately whispers, "This is it. They finally found out I don't know what I'm doing."

This article isn't about "fake it 'til you make it." It's about linguistic re-framing: changing the words you use with yourself and others to build genuine confidence.

The "I Don't Know" Trap

Junior engineers fear saying "I don't know." Senior engineers revel in it. The difference is how they say it.

❌ The Junior Panic

"Uhh, I'm not sure... I think maybe it works like this?" (Voice trails off, sounds uncertain)

✅ The Senior Curiosity

"I haven't encountered that specific edge case before. Let me dig into the logs and I'll share what I find by 2 PM."

Notice the difference? The senior response transforms "ignorance" into an "investigation." It turns a lack of knowledge into a plan of action.

The Dunning-Kruger Effect

Psychologically, imposter syndrome often hits the hardest when you move from "Unconscious Incompetence" (Ignorance is bliss) to "Conscious Incompetence" (You know how much you don't know).

"The more you learn, the more you realize how much you don't know. Feeling stupid is actually a sign of growth."
— Dr. Carol Dweck, Stanford University

3 Scripts to Reframe Your Mindset

1. When You Make a Mistake

Avoid self-deprecation. Do not say "I'm so stupid."

Say instead: "Thanks for catching that. I see now that the race condition was caused by the async hook. I'll add a test case to prevent this in the future."

Why it works: You shift focus from your identity ("I am stupid") to the process ("The hook caused the issue").

2. When You Feel Overwhelmed

Don't suffer in silence to prove you can do it alone.

Say instead: "I've been context-switching between three tickets and I want to ensure quality. Can you help me prioritize which of these is the critical path for launch?"

Why it works: You frame the request as "strategic prioritization" rather than "inability to cope."

3. When You Receive Praise (And Don't Believe It)

Imposters often deflect praise ("Oh, it was nothing, just luck").

Say instead: "Thank you. It was a challenging migration, but I'm really proud of how the team handled the rollback strategy."

Why it works: You accept the credit while acknowledging the difficulty and sharing the win.

Conclusion

Imposter syndrome never fully goes away. Even Principal Engineers feel it when they switch domains. The goal isn't to kill the feeling. The goal is to stop it from silencing you in meetings.

Speak up. Ask the "dumb" question. You'll be surprised how many others in the room were wondering the exact same thing.
