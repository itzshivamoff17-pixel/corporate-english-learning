Answering "Why Should We Hire You?"

The ultimate guide to the most important interview question.

January 18, 2026 · 8 min read

"Why should we hire you?" is the final boss of interview questions. It's asked in 87% of technical interviews (LinkedIn Talent Solutions, 2023), and it's where most candidates fumble.

This isn't a trick question. It's an invitation to sell yourself. But most engineers respond with either false modesty ("I'm a hard worker") or generic buzzwords ("I'm passionate about technology").

This guide will teach you the 3-Part Framework used by successful FAANG candidates to answer this question with confidence.

Why This Question Matters

According to research from Harvard Business School, hiring managers make their final decision based on three factors:

  • Competence: Can you do the job?
  • Cultural Fit: Will you thrive here?
  • Motivation: Do you actually want this job?

Your answer to "Why should we hire you?" must address all three.

The 3-Part Framework

Part 1: The Skill Match (30 seconds)

Connect your specific skills to the job requirements.

"You're looking for a backend engineer with experience in distributed systems. I've spent the last 3 years building microservices at [Company], where I scaled our API to handle 10 million requests per day."

Part 2: The Unique Value (30 seconds)

What makes you different from other candidates?

"What sets me apart is my cross-functional experience. I've worked closely with product and design teams to translate business requirements into technical architecture. This means I can bridge the gap between engineering and stakeholders."

Part 3: The Mutual Fit (20 seconds)

Show you've researched the company and explain why you want this job.

"I'm particularly excited about [Company]'s mission to democratize AI. I've been following your work on [specific project], and I believe my background in ML infrastructure would let me contribute immediately."

Real Example: Junior Engineer

Let's see how a junior engineer with 1 year of experience would answer this question:

❌ Weak Answer

"I'm a hard worker and I'm passionate about coding. I think I'd be a good fit for your team because I'm eager to learn."
✅ Strong Answer

"You're looking for a frontend engineer with React experience. I've built 3 production apps using React at my current company, including a dashboard that reduced customer support tickets by 20%.

What makes me different is my design background—I can implement pixel-perfect UIs without constant back-and-forth with designers.

I'm excited about [Company] because you're solving a real problem in healthcare, and I want to work on products that matter."

Real Example: Senior Engineer

For senior roles, emphasize leadership and impact:

"You need a Staff Engineer who can lead the migration to Kubernetes. I've done this twice before—at [Company A], I led a team of 5 to migrate 50+ services with zero downtime.

What differentiates me is my focus on documentation and knowledge transfer. I don't just build systems; I build teams that can maintain them.

I'm drawn to [Company] because you're at an inflection point—scaling from 100 to 1000 engineers. I thrive in that chaos."

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Being too humble: "I'm not sure if I'm the best, but..." (You're literally being asked to sell yourself. Do it.)
  • Being too arrogant: "I'm the best engineer you'll ever meet." (Confidence, not cockiness.)
  • Repeating your resume: "I have 5 years of experience in Java..." (They already know. Focus on fit.)
  • Generic answers: "I'm a team player." (Everyone says this. Be specific.)

The "Reverse Close" Technique

Advanced move: End your answer with a question that shows confidence.

"...That's why I think I'd be a great fit. But I'm curious—what are the biggest challenges the team is facing right now that I could help solve?"

Why this works: It shifts the dynamic from "interview" to "collaboration." You're no longer begging for the job; you're discussing how to solve their problems together.

Key Takeaways

  • Use the 3-Part Framework: Skill Match + Unique Value + Mutual Fit
  • Keep it under 90 seconds
  • Be specific, not generic
  • Show you've researched the company
