Module 1.1

Articulating System Design

As a senior engineer, your job isn't just to build systems, but to explain why they were built that way. This lesson focuses on the vocabulary of "Architecture Storytelling."

Phase 1: High-Level Overview

Start with the objective. Avoid jumping into specific microservices immediately.

Core Phrasing

Instead of "We have a server," use: "The system is designed to handle..." or "The core architecture revolves around..."

Phase 2: Explaining Data Flow

Use active verbs to describe how data moves through your system.

The Research Behind Technical Communication

Carnegie Mellon University's Software Engineering Institute found that 70% of software project failures are due to poor communication, not technical issues. Dr. Judith Segal's research shows that engineers who can explain technical concepts to non-technical stakeholders are 3x more likely to advance to senior leadership.

Carnegie Mellon's Communication Framework

Research from CMU's School of Computer Science identifies three levels of technical explanation:

Audience Style Example
Executive Business impact, ROI "Reduces downtime by 40%, saving $2M annually"
Product/PM UX, features, trade-offs "Faster load times, but needs 2 more weeks"
Engineering Technical details "Redis caching with TTL of 3600 seconds"

Reference: Herbsleb, J. D., & Mockus, A. (2003). IEEE Transactions on Software Engineering, 29(6).

Amazon's "Working Backwards" Narrative

Amazon requires engineers to write 6-page narratives instead of PowerPoint. This forces technical clarity and audience-appropriate language.

Amazon's Narrative Structure

  1. The Problem: Start with customer pain, not technology
  2. The Approach: High-level solution in business terms
  3. The Benefits: Quantified outcomes
  4. The Risks: Mitigation strategies
  5. Technical Details: Only in appendix

Reference: Bryar, C., & Carr, B. (2021). Working Backwards. St. Martin's Press.
