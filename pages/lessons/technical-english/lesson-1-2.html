System Architecture - Lesson 2

Data Flow Vocabulary

Moving beyond "The data goes here," a professional engineer uses precise verbs to describe the lifecycle of data within a system. This precision is critical for distributed systems.

The Language of Distributed Systems

Google's SRE book and Martin Fowler's architectural guides emphasize that distributed systems introduce complexity that requires specific vocabulary. You can't just say "save" anymore.

The Data Lifecycle Verbs

Ingest
The initial high-speed entry of data from external sources.
"Maximum intake."
"Our Kafka layer ingests 50k events/sec from the IoT sensors."
Propagate
The spread of data/state across nodes or services over time.
"Like a virus."
"Changes to the feature flag are propagated to all regions within 30 seconds."
Aggregate / Reduce
Combining multiple data streams into a single summary.
"Summarizing."
"The analytics engine aggregates raw logs into hourly metrics heaps."
Hydrate
Filling a skeletal object with data from a database.
"Adding water."
"The frontend receives a user ID and hydrates the user profile from the API."
Reconcile
Comparing two datasets and fixing discrepancies.
"Balancing the checkbook."
"A nightly cron job reconciles the payment gateway records with our internal ledger."

Consistency Models: "Stale" vs. "Fresh"

In the era of NoSQL and microservices, "Eventual Consistency" is the norm. Research from Amazon DynamoDB shows that explaining this trade-off is the #1 communication challenge for backend engineers.

Explaining "Eventual Consistency"

The Concept: Data isn't the same everywhere instantly. It takes time to catch up.

The Coffee Shop Analogy

"Imagine you order a latte at Starbucks. The cashier (Write Node) takes your order. The barista (Read Node) doesn't know about it instantly. The cup has to travel down the line. For 30 seconds, the system is 'inconsistent'. But eventually, you get your coffee."

Professional Phrasing

"We accept stale reads for the user profile to maximize performance, but the payment transaction must be strongly consistent."

Reference: Vogels, W. (2009). "Eventually Consistent." ACM Queue.

Event-Driven Architecture (EDA) Patterns

Describing EDA requires specific "Producer/Consumer" vocabulary. ThoughtWorks Technology Radar highlights this as a key literacy for modern devs.

The Standard EDA Sentence Structure

[Subject] emits a [Topic] event, which is consumed by [Service], triggering [Action].

Examples:

  • "The Order Service publishes a 'PaymentSuccess' event."
  • "The Shipping Service subscribes to that topic."
  • "Upon receiving the message, it triggers the label generation workflow."
  • "If the consumer fails, the message is sent to a Dead Letter Queue (DLQ)."
