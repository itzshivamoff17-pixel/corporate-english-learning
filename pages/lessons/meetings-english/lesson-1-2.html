Stand-up Meetings (The Daily Scrum)

Mastering the art of the 2-minute update: Concise, relevant, and unblockable.

The Science of the Stand-up

Jeff Sutherland, co-creator of Scrum, designed the daily stand-up to be short (15 mins max) to induce "active listening" and reduce cognitive load. Research from Scrum.org shows that teams who strictly adhere to timeboxes are 2.5x more likely to deliver sprints on time.

However, Atlassian's "State of Agile" report indicates that 47% of developers dread stand-ups because they devolve into "status reporting" rather than "replanning."

The "Pig vs. Chicken" Metaphor (Scrum lore)

In Scrum, participants are famously divided into two groups based on a fable:

  • Pigs (Committed): The Developers, Scrum Master, Product Owner. They are "committed" to the breakfast (like the pig who provides bacon). They talk.
  • Chickens (Involved): Stakeholders, Managers, Observers. They are only "involved" (like the chicken who provides eggs). They listen but do not interrupt.

Key Lesson: If you are a "Chicken," save your questions for the "Parking Lot" after the meeting.

Reference: Sutherland, J. (2014). Scrum: The Art of Doing Twice the Work in Half the Time. Crown Business.

The 3-Question Framework (Standard Scrum)

The classic format focuses on alignment, not status. It’s about flow.

The 3 Core Questions

  • 1. What did I do yesterday that helped the Development Team meet the Sprint Goal?
    ❌ "I wrote code."
    ✅ "I committed the user authentication module, which unblocks the frontend team."
  • 2. What will I do today to help the Development Team meet the Sprint Goal?
    ❌ "Same as yesterday."
    ✅ "I'm focusing on the JWT token validation. I aim to have a PR open by 3 PM."
  • 3. Do I see any impediment that prevents me or the Development Team from meeting the Sprint Goal?
    ❌ "No blockers." (When you're actually stuck waiting for designs)
    ✅ "Yes, I'm currently blocked by the missing Figma assets for the login modal."

Walk the Board (Modern Kanban Style)

Many mature agile teams (like those at Spotify and Atlassian) have moved away from "What I did" to "Walk the Board." Instead of going person-by-person, you go ticket-by-ticket from Right to Left (Done -> In Progress -> To Do).

Why "Walk the Board"?

Focusing on the work rather than the worker reduces social anxiety and emphasizes team ownership.

  • Focus: "This ticket is stuck in QA. Who can help move it?"
  • Collaboration: "This item is taking longer than expected. Does anyone have capacity to pair program?"

Reference: Anderson, D. J. (2010). Kanban: Successful Evolutionary Change for Your Technology Business. Blue Hole Press.

Common Anti-Patterns (What to Avoid)

Jason Yip, Agile Coach at Spotify, identifies these common "smells" of a bad stand-up:

1. The Status Report

Talking to the Manager instead of the team.

Fix: Look at your teammates, not the boss.

2. The Problem Solver

Diving into deep technical discussions.

Fix: "Let's take this offline" or "Let's discuss in the Parking Lot."

Phrases for "The Parking Lot"

Use these phrases to politely defer off-topic discussions:

  • "This sounds important, but it might take more than 2 minutes. Can we park this for after the stand-up?"
  • "I think this requires a deeper dive. Let's sync up directly after the meeting."
  • "Good point. I'll add that to the Parking Lot list."
