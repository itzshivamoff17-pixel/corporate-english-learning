Visual Design for Engineers

Technical presentations often fail because the slides are "Walls of Text." Your slides should not be your teleprompter. This lesson covers data visualization principles from Edward Tufte and Nancy Duarte.

The "Data-Ink Ratio" (Edward Tufte)

Yale Professor Edward Tufte, the pioneer of data visualization, coined the term Data-Ink Ratio. It means: "Remove any ink that does not convey information."

Chart Junk Removal

❌ Low Ratio (Bad)

  • 3D pie charts (Distorts data)
  • Heavy grid lines
  • Decorative backgrounds
  • "Cool" animations

✅ High Ratio (Good)

  • Simple bar charts
  • Direct labeling (No legends)
  • White background
  • Highlights only on key insights

Reference: Tufte, E. R. (2001). The Visual Display of Quantitative Information. Graphics Press.

The "3-Second Rule" (Nancy Duarte)

Nancy Duarte, who designed Al Gore's An Inconvenient Truth, states that an audience member should understand the point of a slide within 3 seconds. If they have to read, they stop listening to you.

The Glance Test

Stand 5 feet back from your monitor. Can you read the headline? Does the graphic speak for itself?

Structure:
1. Headline: The main takeaway (e.g., "Latency dropped by 40%")
2. Body: The evidence (The chart)
3. Footer: Source/Caveats

Slidedocs: The detailed Hybrid

Sometimes you need to convey dense technical details. Don't put it on the screen. Create a Slidedoc—a visual document intended to be read, not presented.

Strategy: "I have sent ahead a detailed Slidedoc with the architecture diagrams. For this meeting, I will only present the high-level decision flow."

Live Demo Etiquette

Demos are the ultimate proof of work. But they are also high-risk. Follow these linguistic rules to keep the audience engaged even when things go slow.

  • Narrate the Action: "As you can see, I am now initializing the script..."
  • Fill the 'Wait': "While this build completes (it usually takes 30s), I want to highlight the [X] configuration."
  • Handle the 'Fail': "It seems we have a connection timeout. No worries, I had a fallback environment ready for exactly this reason."

References & Further Reading

  2. Duarte, N. (2008). slide:ology: The Art and Science of Creating Great Presentations. O'Reilly Media.
  3. Reynolds, G. (2008). Presentation Zen. New Riders.
